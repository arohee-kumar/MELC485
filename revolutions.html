Green Movement

The Green Movement was a political movement that started on June 12, 2009 after the Iranian presidential elections and lasted until 2010. The incumbent President Mahmoud Ahmadinejad was running for re-election after being elected for the first time in 2005. The first time President Ahmadinejad ran, he promised the people that "[He] will better distribute the existing wealth." and "[He]'ll bring the oil money to the people's tables." He didn't deliver on his campaign promises of "combating corruption; improving the economy; dealing with issues of social justice and unemployment, especially for the young; and creating greater opportunities for people." During his term the economy got worse, Iranian people had to deal with more international sanctions, and higher inflation rates. The people wanted this to change and challenged the outcome of the election.

The Revolution of 1979 also played a part in the Green Revolution. The majority of the Iranian public was involved: the middle class, the poor, the women's groups, the student groups, members of the bazaar, and people of the new industrialist class. A faction of these people were led by Ayatollah Ruhollah Khomeini, Mir Hossein Mousavi and his wife Zahra Rahnavard. Another revolutionary coalition was made up of Mahmoud Ahmadinejad, the Revolutionary Guards (IRGC), and Basij. These revolutionary group was able to overthrow the Shah Mohammad Reza Pahlavi. Ayatollah Khomeini then served as the first supreme leader of Iran. Khomeini didn't allow the IRGC to be included in politics, but once he passed and Ali Khamenei took place as the second supreme leader, the IRGC was allowed to become involved.



Arab Spring - Egypt

The Arab Spring was a large movement that started in Tunisia and then moved to Bahrain, Egypt, Libya, Syria, and Yemen. The Arab Spring in Egypt started on January 16, 2011 after Abdou Abdel-Monaam Hamadah set himself on fire to protest unreasonable bread prices. Inspired by him and the protester from Tunisia, Mohamed Bouazizi, multiple self-immolated in front of the Egyptian parliament building in Cairo to protest dire living conditions, dissatisfaction with the President Hosni Mubarak, unemployment, and police brutality.

President Mubarak was the vice president for President Anwar el-Sadat and gained his presidency in 1981 after President Sadat was assassinated by attackers from an Islamic militant group. His first action was to restrict political expression, expand police powers, and enact media censorship. He was reelected in 1987, 1993, and 1999 without serious challengers. In 2005, he faced two main candidates to run against. The Muslim Brotherhood was banned from entering a candidate. Once Mubarak won, the runner-up, Ayman Nour, was imprisoned on charges of fraud. Independent candidates who were associated with the banned Muslim Brotherhood joined the People's Assembly, they called for the results of the election to be annulled by claiming that the voting was rigged.
