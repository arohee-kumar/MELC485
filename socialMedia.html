Blogging in Egypt

From The Blogosphere To The Street: Social Media and Egyptian Revolution does a great job of explaining how blogging helped the public make their voice hear in Egypt. Blogging became popular in Egypt around 2004. It was used to have a platform where politcal and activist conversations could occur between people that don't agree on fundamental ideas. People from the Muslim Brotherhood and secular-liberal ones were having productive conversations on how they dislike and reject the Mubarak regime. The blog platform removed the debate of secularism vs. fundamentalism and allowed people to debate on the issues that were actually affecting them. The language used deviated from what was considered "standard". Rather than using formal Arabic, bloggers used the Egyptian dialect. This may have led to the people feeling more connected to each other and in turn to the issues that they all were facing with the Mubarak regime. By using colloquial and vulgar language, blogs were able to get the attention of secular-leftists and Islamists because it was so different from the formal language used by the oppressive political institutions.

Role of Media in Revolutions

Iran

Because social media played such a large role in the Green Movement, it is also known as the Twitter Revolution and the Facebook Movement. Following the election of President Ahmadinejad, his opponent Mir Hossein Mousavi's supporters started attacks on his site and the tools to hack the site were shared over Twitter and Facebook. In retaliation the government stopped Internet access. When the election results were shared, the Internet was shut down for a brief period and then restarted, most likely to set filters on YouTube and Facebook.

Egypt

There was a rise in censorship leading up to the Arab Spring. Egypt. On January 25th, 2011, Twitter was blocked by State Security Investigation Service. The next day Facebook was shut down. Finally, during the night of January 27th, The government shut down SMS and the Internet. Mubarak increased security and the firm that monitors the Internet in Egypt reports nearly all routes to Egyptian networks were taken down at the same time.

Instead protestors used back channels like WhatsApp and email platforms to communicate. Primarily depending on Google, Microsoft, and Yahoo to connect to one another. BBC states that "when one channel of communication is blocked, people try another." People were using mobile phones, placards, newspapers, leaflets, messages carved into stones, and al-Jazeera's TV cameras.
